III

The Masters

Custodians of a craft, named and remembered.

We do not weave anonymously. Every Varenyam creation carries the name of the hands that made it.

Master Weaver

Rajendra Prasad

LineageThird Generation
SpecialtyKadhwa Brocade
Years at Loom41

Rajendra learned the loom from his father, who learned it from his own — a continuous line stretching back to the early twentieth century in the lanes of Madanpura. He is regarded among collectors as one of the last living masters of the kadhwa technique, in which each motif is woven separately into the base fabric rather than floated across it.

"My grandfather used to say a sari should be heavier with history than with thread," he recalls. "I still weigh every piece by that measure before it leaves my loom."

Master Weaver

Husna Begum

LineageSecond Generation
SpecialtyZari & Tissue Work
Years at Loom27

Husna is one of the few women to lead her own karkhana in Varanasi, having taken over her father's looms after his passing. Her tissue-work — a near-translucent weave shot through with gold — is among the most technically demanding forms still practiced in the city.

She trains four apprentices, two of them her own daughters, in a deliberate effort to keep the technique from disappearing within her lifetime.

Master Weaver

Vinod Kumar Sahu

LineageFourth Generation
SpecialtyJangla & Shikargah Motifs
Years at Loom33

Vinod's family has woven jangla — dense, foliage-inspired patterns — since the 1890s, when his great-grandfather first recorded the motif's structure on graph paper still kept in the family home. He is one of perhaps a dozen weavers in Varanasi who can still execute a full shikargah hunting scene by hand.

"Every motif I weave is a letter to someone who isn't born yet," he says. "I weave it carefully because I won't be there to explain it."
