The Hands Behind the Masterpieces

Profiles11 Min ReadThe Editorial Desk

There is a particular quiet that settles over a karkhana in the hour before dusk, when the day's weaving slows and a master pauses to examine, thread by thread, what the loom has produced since morning. It is in this quiet that the real story of Varenyam is told — not in our showroom, because we don't have one, but in the workshops of Varanasi where each creation actually comes into being.

We spent the better part of a year visiting three such workshops, observing the masters whose names now appear on every Varenyam creation's certificate of provenance.

Rajendra Prasad — The Third Generation

Rajendra's workshop in Madanpura has occupied the same narrow building since his grandfather's time. He is, by his own estimate, one of fewer than fifteen weavers left in the city who can execute a full kadhwa brocade — a technique in which each motif is woven as a distinct unit rather than carried across the fabric, resulting in a sari with no loose threads on its reverse side at all.

Husna Begum — Leading Her Own Looms

When Husna's father passed, custom in her community suggested the karkhana would pass to a male relative. Husna fought to keep it, and now runs four looms herself, training her two daughters in tissue-zari work alongside two unrelated apprentices — an act she describes simply as "making sure the knowledge has somewhere to go."

Vinod Kumar Sahu — Keeper of the Shikargah

Vinod's family has kept a handwritten graph-paper record of their jangla and shikargah motifs since the 1890s, water-damaged at the edges but still legible. He showed us the page used to weave Creation No. IX, tracing the original pencil lines with a finger before describing, almost apologetically, how long it takes him now to see well enough to follow them.

Each of these masters works independently of any single brand, including ours. What Varenyam offers is not exclusivity over their craft, but a commitment to attribute it honestly, compensate it fairly, and present it without dilution — a small set of conditions that, in this industry, are not nearly as common as they should be.
